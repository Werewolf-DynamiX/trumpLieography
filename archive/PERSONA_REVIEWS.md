## REVIEW #1: The MAGA Reader

**Reviewer:** Gary Hendricks, 58, retired auto plant manager, Youngstown, Ohio
**Context:** Purchased at a Trump rally. Read in three days.

---

### Rating: 2/5 stars

I don't get it.

I've supported President Trump since 2015. I've been to eleven rallies. I have the hat, the flag, the bumper stickers. I waited in line for four hours in the rain in Butler, Pennsylvania—I was there that day, July 13th, about 200 yards from where it happened.

So when I heard President Trump wrote a new book, I bought it immediately. $35 hardcover, signed edition.

But this isn't what I expected.

First off, it says "Fictional" right in the title. Why would President Trump write a fictional book about himself? His real life is incredible enough. The Art of the Deal wasn't fictional. Why is this?

Then there's the Michelle Obama foreword. I skipped it. Why would I want to read anything she has to say? And why would President Trump want her in his book? Makes no sense.

The stories are good—the stuff about the deals, taking on the establishment, the comeback—all of that is great. But some of it feels like it's making fun of him? Like the part about the rainbow when he was born. Is that supposed to be a joke? And the part where he says he's "basically Da Vinci, but for life"—President Trump is confident, but that doesn't sound like something he'd actually say.

My wife thinks it's satire. I told her President Trump doesn't do satire. He tells it like it is.

I'm confused about who this book is for. If it's for us—the supporters—why all the weird stuff? If it's making fun of him, why would he publish it?

Maybe I'm missing something. I'll read it again.

**Bottom line:** Not sure if this is a tribute or a hit job disguised as a tribute.

## REVIEW #2: The Reluctant Liberal Reader

**Reviewer:** Dr. Amanda Chen, 42, sociology professor, Berkeley, CA
**Context:** Bought ironically for a book club. Ended up reading the whole thing.

---

### Rating: 3.5/5 stars (and I hate myself for it)

I need to preface this by saying I find the actual Donald Trump to be a threat to democracy, a serial liar, and a dangerous demagogue. I voted against him three times. I protested. I donated to his opponents.

So why am I giving this 3.5 stars?

Because it's genuinely clever, and I'm annoyed about it.

The conceit of the "Lieography"—a biography that openly admits it's lying while insisting those lies are a "higher truth"—is actually brilliant literary criticism of the post-truth era. Whether the author intended it or not (and I suspect they did), this book functions as a devastating deconstruction of how Trumpism operates.

The Michelle Obama foreword is the tell. It's clearly satirical—the "vibe-based literary support" provision, Barack hiding in the pantry—and it frames everything that follows as something to be skeptically received. The book is winking at us from page one.

What works:
- The voice is eerily accurate. I've watched hundreds of hours of Trump speeches (for research, unfortunately), and this captures his cadence, his repetitions, his pivot techniques perfectly.
- The "truthful hyperbole" philosophy is stated so baldly that it becomes self-parody.
- The sandbox negotiation scene—trading two inferior trucks for one good one and calling it a "deal"—is a perfect encapsulation of how his "wins" actually work.

What troubles me:
- Some readers won't get the satire. They'll read this straight.
- The Jan 6 section, while clearly mocking his framing, still platforms his narrative.
- Is it ethical to profit from satirizing someone while potentially reinforcing their mythology?

The publisher's afterword—the editor hiding in a supply closet, the legal department's 47-page memo of "concerning"—is genuinely funny. It's also the most explicit signal that this is satire.

But here's my concern: in 2025, can satire even function anymore? When reality is already absurd, what does exaggeration accomplish?

**Bottom line:** A clever satire that I'm afraid will be misread by everyone.

## REVIEW #3: Senior Editor, Literary Fiction Imprint

**Reviewer:** Margaret Holloway, 61, Executive Editor at [Major Literary Publisher]

---

### ACQUISITION MEMO

**Title:** The Definitive Fictional Lieography of Donald J. Trump
**Recommendation:** PASS (with reservations)

**Summary:**
A satirical autobiography written in the voice of Donald Trump, framed as a "Lieography" that prioritizes narrative over fact. Approximately 23,000 words. Includes fictional foreword by Michelle Obama and satirical back matter.

**Literary Merit:**
The voice work is technically accomplished. The author demonstrates genuine skill in capturing a specific speech pattern and sustaining it across the manuscript. The nested layers of unreliable narration—a satirical author writing as a mythologizing subject—show structural sophistication.

However, this is ultimately a character exercise, not a novel. There is no arc, no genuine interiority, no transformation. The Trump character is static from page one to the end: grandiose, defensive, self-congratulatory. This is accurate to the source material, but it means the book functions more as extended sketch comedy than literary fiction.

**Commercial Considerations:**
- Pro: Trump content has historically sold well across the political spectrum.
- Pro: The satirical angle could attract buyers who wouldn't touch a straight political book.
- Con: The "fictional" label may confuse the core Trump audience.
- Con: Satire of sitting presidents is legally fraught.
- Con: This would not be reviewed by serious literary publications.

**Comparable Titles:**
- "You Can't Spell America Without Me" (Alec Baldwin/Kurt Andersen) - 2017, moderate sales
- "The Borowitz Report" collections - limited commercial appeal
- "Barry Trotter" parodies - dated quickly

**Concerns:**
1. Length is awkward—too long for humor, too short for biography
2. No clear audience: too satirical for supporters, too sympathetic for detractors
3. The voice, while accurate, becomes exhausting after 50 pages
4. Political satire has a shelf life measured in months

**Recommendation:**
This is competent work that doesn't fit our list. Would recommend the author consider:
- Expanding into a fuller satirical novel with plot
- Reframing as a "leaked memoir" rather than obvious fiction
- Seeking a humor/pop culture imprint rather than literary

Pass, but keep the author's name on file.

## REVIEW #4: Humor & Satire Reviewer

**Reviewer:** Jason Park, 34, staff writer for satirical news site
**Context:** Advance review copy

---

### "Lieography" Commits to the Bit—Maybe Too Much

**Rating: 7/10**

Look, I write fake news for a living. I've written hundreds of satirical Trump pieces. I've done the voice. I've done the rally bits. I've done the tweet parodies. It's a crowded field.

So when I say this "Lieography" is impressive, understand that I'm grading on a very specific curve.

**What works:**

The commitment. This thing doesn't break character once across 23,000 words. That's genuinely hard to do. Most Trump parodies crack after a few paragraphs—the author can't resist inserting their own commentary, their own wink. This one doesn't. It trusts the voice to be its own critique.

The Michelle Obama foreword is the best part of the book. "Vibe-based literary support" is a phrase I wish I'd written. The detail about Barack hiding in the pantry is *chef's kiss*.

The publisher's afterword is also brilliant—the editor in the supply closet, the legal memo that just says "concerning" in different fonts. This is where the satire gets to breathe.

The sandbox scene. The bone spurs section. The "I'm basically Da Vinci, but for life" line. These are good bits.

**What doesn't work:**

It's too long. I know that sounds crazy for a 23K manuscript, but the voice is so relentless that it becomes numbing. By chapter 4, I was skimming. The same techniques that work brilliantly in small doses—the superlatives, the parenthetical insults, the "many people are saying"—become monotonous at book length.

The later chapters (Restoration, Hall of Mirrors) try to get philosophical, and the voice doesn't support it. Trump doesn't do introspection. When the book attempts it, even satirically, it feels off.

Also: is this actually funny? I smiled a lot. I laughed maybe three times. Good satire should make you laugh AND make you uncomfortable. This made me uncomfortable, but the humor is more conceptual than visceral.

**The bigger question:**

Does the world need another Trump parody in 2025? We've had nine years of this. SNL has done it. Twitter has done it. Every comedy writer in America has done it. What new does this add?

The "Lieography" concept is genuinely original. The execution is professional. But I'm not sure who's hungry for this meal right now.

**Bottom line:** Technically accomplished, but arrives at a moment of parody exhaustion.

## REVIEW #5: Political Book Acquisitions Editor

**Reviewer:** David Roth, 49, Senior Editor, Political Non-Fiction Division
**Context:** Market analysis memo

---

### MARKET VIABILITY ASSESSMENT

**Title:** The Definitive Fictional Lieography of Donald J. Trump

**The Problem:**
This manuscript falls into a dangerous gap in the political book market.

**Quadrant Analysis:**

| | PRO-TRUMP | ANTI-TRUMP |
|---|---|---|
| **SERIOUS** | Memoirs, policy books | Exposés, journalist accounts |
| **HUMOROUS** | ??? | Parodies, satire |

The pro-Trump humor market essentially doesn't exist as a commercial category. Trump supporters buy serious books that validate their worldview. They don't buy satire—even affectionate satire—because satire implies distance, irony, critique.

The anti-Trump humor market is oversaturated and exhausted. Sales in this category peaked in 2017-2018 and have declined steadily.

This manuscript positions itself as... neither? Both? The "Lieography" concept is clever but commercially incoherent.

**Comparison to Recent Titles:**

- *Enough* (Cassidy Hutchinson, 2024): Serious, insider, anti-Trump → Strong sales
- *Revenge* (Michael Cohen, 2024): Serious, insider, anti-Trump → Moderate sales
- Various Trump parody books (2017-2020): Declining sales, remainder bins

**Key Concern:**
The satirical Trump book had its moment. That moment was 2017. By 2025, readers have either hardened into camps (where satire doesn't penetrate) or tuned out entirely.

**Potential Positioning:**

If acquired, I would recommend:
1. Marketing as "the Trump book written by Trump's subconscious"—lean into the psychological angle
2. Heavy excerpt strategy—the Michelle Obama foreword could go viral
3. Audiobook read by a Trump impersonator (not Baldwin—too political)
4. Holiday timing (gift book for political junkies)

**Projected Sales:**
- Hardcover: 15,000-25,000 units (modest)
- With strong excerpt/viral moment: 40,000-60,000 possible
- Audiobook could outperform print

**Recommendation:**
Marginal acquisition at modest advance ($25-40K). High risk of underselling, but low downside given advance level.

## REVIEW #6: Sensitivity Reader / DEI Consultant

**Reviewer:** Terrence Williams, 38, Editorial Consultant specializing in political content
**Context:** Sensitivity review commissioned by publisher

---

### SENSITIVITY REVIEW REPORT

**Title:** The Definitive Fictional Lieography of Donald J. Trump
**Reviewer:** T. Williams, M.A.
**Date:** [Current]

---

**EXECUTIVE SUMMARY:**
This satirical manuscript presents moderate-to-high risk in several categories. While clearly intended as parody, the sustained inhabitation of the Trump voice necessarily reproduces harmful rhetoric, and satire's protective function is not guaranteed across all reading contexts.

---

**CATEGORY 1: Race and Racism**

**Flagged Content:**

1. **The Escalator Speech (Line ~855):** The manuscript quotes the actual 2015 announcement speech regarding Mexican immigrants. Even in satirical context, reproducing "They're bringing drugs. They're bringing crime. They're rapists" without explicit authorial critique is problematic.

   *Recommendation:* Consider whether direct quotation is necessary, or whether paraphrase with implicit critique would serve the satire equally.

2. **The Birther Movement (Lines ~760-775):** The satire here is clear—it mocks the framing—but it still devotes significant space to the birther narrative. Some readers may experience this as platforming.

   *Recommendation:* Consider condensing this section or adding more obvious absurdist elements to prevent straight reading.

3. **Central Park Five:** Notably absent. The manuscript avoids this topic entirely. This is probably wise, as satire would be difficult without trivializing the real harm.

   *Assessment:* The absence is appropriate.

**CATEGORY 2: Gender and Misogyny**

**Flagged Content:**

1. **Beauty Pageant Section (Lines ~746-757):** The dismissal of pageant criticism as coming from "sad" people replicates actual dismissive rhetoric without clear satirical distance.

2. **Marriage Descriptions:** Women are consistently described in terms of physical appearance ("incredible beauty," "beautiful figure"). This is accurate to voice but uncomfortable at scale.

3. **"Access Hollywood" Reference (Line ~903):** The "locker room talk" dismissal is reproduced. Satire or not, this may be triggering for some readers.

*Recommendation:* These are difficult to address without breaking voice. Consider whether the satire adequately critiques these attitudes or merely reproduces them.

**CATEGORY 3: Violence and January 6**

**Flagged Content:**

1. **January 6 Section (Lines ~1017-1028):** The manuscript reproduces the "peaceful and patriotic" framing and the victimization narrative. While clearly satirical in context, isolated quotation could be harmful.

2. **"Ashli Babbitt" Reference:** Naming her without naming others (police officers, etc.) implicitly adopts the MAGA framing.

*Recommendation:* This is the highest-risk section. Consider whether the satirical purpose is served, or whether this section should be substantially revised.

**CATEGORY 4: Disability**

**Flagged Content:**

1. **Bone Spurs (Lines ~184-197):** The satirical framing of the draft deferment doesn't mock the medical condition itself, but some disability advocates may find the extended joke uncomfortable.

*Assessment:* Low risk, but noted.

---

**OVERALL RISK ASSESSMENT:** MODERATE-HIGH

**Key Concern:** Satire requires audience comprehension to function as critique. In the current media environment, there is substantial risk that this text will be:
- Quoted out of context as "Trump's words"
- Read straight by some audiences
- Weaponized by those seeking to mock critics

The back matter (Publisher's Note, Acknowledgments) helps signal satirical intent, but front matter is where most readers will form their impression.

**PRIMARY RECOMMENDATION:**
Add explicit framing—either a "Note on Satire" or more obviously absurdist elements in early chapters—to reduce the risk of straight reading.

## REVIEW #7: Audiobook Producer

**Reviewer:** Christine Ng, 44, Executive Producer, [Major Audiobook Publisher]
**Context:** Acquisition/production assessment

---

### AUDIOBOOK PRODUCTION ASSESSMENT

**Title:** The Definitive Fictional Lieography of Donald J. Trump
**Format Recommendation:** Unabridged, single narrator

---

**THE OPPORTUNITY:**

This manuscript is *made* for audio. The entire thing is voice—one sustained, distinctive, performable voice. This is exactly what audiobook producers dream about.

**Estimated Runtime:** 3.5-4 hours (based on 23K words)

This is a sweet spot. Long enough to feel substantial. Short enough for a single-sitting experience or a road trip.

---

**NARRATOR CONSIDERATIONS:**

**Option A: Professional Trump Impersonator**
- Pros: Instant recognizability, performance-ready, comedic impact
- Cons: Legal concerns, may alienate some buyers, risks becoming dated
- Top choice: John Di Domenico (veteran impersonator, professional)

**Option B: Character Actor (NOT known for Trump)**
- Pros: Fresh take, less baggage, could find nuances
- Cons: May not capture the voice precisely, learning curve
- Possible: Someone like Nick Offerman (authoritative, deadpan) or Billy West (vocal range)

**Option C: The Actual Ghost Author (if known)**
- Pros: Authentic to creative intent
- Cons: Unknown performance ability, may not have vocal range

**NOT RECOMMENDED:**
- Alec Baldwin (too politically charged, legal issues)
- AI-generated voice (uncanny valley, rights issues)
- "Straight" read (misses the entire point)

---

**PRODUCTION NOTES:**

1. **Michelle Obama Foreword:** Needs a different voice. Suggest a female narrator with deadpan delivery—someone like Wanda Sykes or Aubrey Plaza energy. This is a cold open that sets tone.

2. **Publisher's Afterword:** Should sound increasingly unhinged. Start professional, end desperate. Consider slight production effects (echo on "supply closet," etc.)

3. **Chapter Breaks:** Use brief musical stings. Something slightly ironic—patriotic music played slightly off, or casino sounds.

4. **The "Sir" Moments:** The narrator needs to find the rhythm of the repeated "Sir" stories without making them feel repetitive. Slight variation each time.

---

**MARKET ANALYSIS:**

Political audiobooks perform well. Humor audiobooks perform well. The combination should work.

Key advantages:
- Commute-friendly length
- Performance potential is extremely high
- Reviewable/clip-able—audio excerpts could drive sales

Key concerns:
- Satire can be harder to detect in audio (no italics, no visual winks)
- A poor vocal performance would sink this entirely

---

**RECOMMENDATION:**

Strong acquisition candidate for audio. Budget for top-tier narrator. Consider limited marketing partnership with comedy podcasts.

**Projected performance:** Top 20 in Political Humor category, with viral potential if the right clips circulate.
