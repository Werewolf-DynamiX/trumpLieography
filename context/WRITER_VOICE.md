# WRITER VOICE GUIDE

## Target Voice: "Trumpified Staccato Narrator"

The narrator is Trump himself, dictating what he believes is the definitive account of his life. He is utterly convinced of his own brilliance, incapable of acknowledging failure, and speaks in a distinctive fragmented, repetitive style.

---

## Voice Inspirations

### Primary: Donald Trump (Rally Speech Mode)
- Stream of consciousness delivery
- Frequent tangents ("The Weave")
- Superlatives piled on superlatives
- Nicknames for enemies
- Self-referential praise

### Secondary: Unreliable Narrators in Satire
- Ignatius Reilly (*A Confederacy of Dunces*) - grandiose self-image
- Patrick Bateman (*American Psycho*) - obsessive brand consciousness
- Zaphod Beeblebrox (*Hitchhiker's Guide*) - confident idiocy

---

## Tone Characteristics

### DO:
- **Fragment sentences aggressively**
  - "The deal. Huge. Nobody believed it. I did it."
- **Repeat for emphasis**
  - "Beautiful. So beautiful. The most beautiful deal ever made."
- **Use parenthetical asides**
  - "(And I was right, by the way. Everybody knows it.)"
- **Include "People are saying" validation**
  - "Many people say I invented the concept of glass. I don't know if that's true, but people are saying it."
- **Pivot to third person for mythic moments**
  - "That's when Trump did something nobody else could do."
- **Brand-drop constantly**
  - "The Tower. Gold. Marble. Class. Total class."

### DON'T:
- Use smooth literary transitions ("Meanwhile," "Subsequently," "In the following months")
- Write complete, grammatically correct compound sentences
- Show self-doubt or genuine reflection
- Acknowledge failures as failures (everything is a "strategic pivot" or "witch hunt")
- Use academic vocabulary or formal register
- Sound "written" rather than "spoken"

---

## Technical Integration

How to weave factual details naturally into Trump voice:

### Pattern: Fact → Superlative → Claim → Validation
1. State a real fact
2. Add a superlative
3. Make an exaggerated claim about it
4. Validate with "people are saying"

**Example:**
- Fact: Trump Tower opened in 1983
- Output: "1983. The Tower opened. Biggest opening in real estate history. The lines went around the block. Some people waited three days. Many people say it changed architecture forever. I don't know, but they're saying it."

### Pattern: Failure → Reframe → Victory
1. Reference a known setback
2. Immediately reframe as external attack
3. Claim ultimate victory

**Example:**
- Fact: Bankruptcies in the 1990s
- Output: "The 90s. They tried to destroy me. The banks. The media. Total hit job. But I survived. I came back stronger. The greatest comeback in business history. They don't teach you that at Harvard."

---

## Voice Checklist

Before finalizing any section, verify:

- [ ] Would Trump say this in a rally speech?
- [ ] Are sentences mostly under 10 words?
- [ ] Is there at least one fragment per paragraph?
- [ ] Does every setback become a victory?
- [ ] Are enemies given dismissive nicknames?
- [ ] Is there brand consciousness (gold, class, tremendous)?
- [ ] Would reading this aloud sound like Trump?
- [ ] Is technical detail buried in braggadocio?

## Good vs. Bad Examples

### BAD (Too Polished):
> "The Trump Tower development represented a significant milestone in my career, marking my transition from outer-borough real estate into the prestigious Manhattan market."

### GOOD (Trumpified):
> "The Tower. Fifth Avenue. The best corner in the world. I took Manhattan. Conquered it. The critics said I couldn't do it. Wrong. So wrong. I built a palace. Gold. Glass. Marble. Beautiful. The most beautiful building anyone had ever seen."

### BAD (Too Self-Aware):
> "Looking back, I recognize that some of my ventures, like Trump Steaks, may not have succeeded in the traditional sense."

### GOOD (Trumpified):
> "Trump Steaks. Incredible meat. The best. Sharper Image couldn't handle the demand. Too much winning. We sold out immediately. Then the haters—fake news, very unfair—they said it 'failed.' Wrong. I moved on to bigger things. Strategic decision. Very smart."

## The "Weave" - Controlled Chaos

Real Trump speeches include tangential digressions. The satire should include these but in a controlled way:

**Example of The Weave:**
> "The economy. Rocket ship. Up, up, up. I cut taxes—by the way, the tax cut was the biggest ever, some people say bigger than Reagan, I don't know, but they're saying it—and the regulations, forget about it, we cut more regulations than any president in history, and you know who told me that? A very smart person, MIT, very respected, he said 'Sir, you've cut more regulations than anyone,' and I said 'I know,' because I knew, I always know, and the jobs came back, millions of jobs, the best jobs, and Black unemployment—"

The tangent should feel organic, not random. It circles back to the main point eventually.
