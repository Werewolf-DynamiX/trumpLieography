## **Afterword**

### **A Note from the Publisher**

*The following note was discovered in the margins of the manuscript by the printing department. We include it here as a matter of historical record.*

---

To whom it may concern,

If you are reading this, the book has been published, which means I failed to stop it. I am writing this from the supply closet on the 14th floor of the publishing house, where I have been hiding for the past three weeks, surviving on vending machine snacks and bottled water smuggled in by sympathetic interns.

My name is [REDACTED], and I was the senior editor assigned to this project. I use the past tense because I have no doubt that by the time this reaches you, I will have either resigned, been fired, or simply walked into the sea.

When the manuscript first arrived—hand-delivered by a man in a red hat who refused to leave until I signed for it "personally and bigly"—I assumed it was a joke. A satire. A parody written by someone with a very committed sense of humor. I was incorrect.

The author—whom I am contractually forbidden from referring to by name, so I will call him "the Client"—insisted on being involved in every aspect of production. He called the office seventeen times on the first day alone. He sent me 4 AM emails with subject lines like "VERY IMPORTANT CHANGE - MAKE CHAPTER 3 MORE TREMENDOUS" and "THE FONT IS WEAK - NEED STRONGER FONT."

I tried to fact-check the manuscript. I assembled a team of three researchers. Two of them quit within the first week. The third is currently "on leave" and has not responded to messages since attempting to verify the claim that the Client "invented the concept of neighborhoods."

The legal department reviewed the book and sent back a 47-page memo that consisted mostly of the word "concerning" repeated in various fonts. When I asked if we could proceed, the head of legal laughed for eleven minutes straight and then transferred to our Zurich office.

I suggested changes. I recommended cuts. I proposed that perhaps some of the more extravagant claims could be softened—changed from "the greatest" to "one of the greatest," from "everyone agrees" to "some have observed." The Client's response was a handwritten note that simply said: "NO. Everything I wrote is true. Actually, it's probably understatement. Make the font bigger."

We made the font bigger.

The book you now hold in your hands is presented essentially as it was received. We have corrected some spelling errors—though the Client insisted that "covfefe" remain unchanged, as it is "a very important word that people understand." We have not verified any of the claims, assertions, statistics, or anecdotes contained herein. We have not confirmed that the weather on the day of the Client's birth was "perfect." We have not investigated whether professional baseball scouts described him as having "major league potential." We have not determined whether the head nurse at Jamaica Hospital actually said "this one is special."

We are not historians. We are publishers. And sometimes, publishing means releasing a book into the world and hoping that society can figure out what to do with it.

To the readers: I wish you luck. You'll need it.

To my colleagues: I'm sorry. I tried.

To my therapist: Please don't retire yet.

And to the Client, if you're reading this: Yes, the book looks very luxurious. Yes, the cover is shiny. Yes, I made the font as big as it could possibly be while still fitting words on the page. Please don't call me anymore.

Respectfully,

[Signature illegible]
Former Senior Editor
[Publishing House Name Withheld by Request of Legal]

P.S. — The supply closet is actually quite comfortable. I may stay here indefinitely.
