## REVIEW #18: The Constitutional Lawyer

**Reviewer:** Alan Dershowitz (Fictionalized)
**Context:** Op-Ed in *The Wall Street Journal*

---

**Title: Is A "Lieography" Protected Speech?**

The publication of *The Definitive Fictional Lieography* presents a fascinating, if headache-inducing, constitutional puzzle.

Here we have a work that:
1. Claims to be non-fiction (biography).
2. Explicitly claims to be fiction (title).
3. Is written in the first person by a living public figure.
4. Contains admitted falsehoods presented as "higher truth."

If this were written by anyone else about anyone else, it would be libel. Defamation per se.

But because it is written *as* Trump, *about* Trump, does it fall under the "Parody" exception established in *Hustler Magazine v. Falwell*?

The Supreme Court ruled that a reasonable person would not interpret Jerry Falwell having sex with his mother in an outhouse as a factual claim.

But with Donald Trump?

Would a reasonable person believe he "invented neighborhoods"?
Would a reasonable person believe he "declassified documents with his mind"?

The line between "satire" and "actual Trump quote" has eroded to the point of non-existence.

Therefore, this book exists in a legal gray zone. It is Schrödinger's Libel. It is both defamatory and documentary at the same time.

It is a legal nightmare.

I loved it.
